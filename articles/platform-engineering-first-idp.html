Platform Engineering: Building Your First Internal Developer Platform

Every company over ~30 engineers eventually rebuilds the same things: service scaffolding, CI/CD, secrets, observability. Platform engineering is the discipline of building those once, well, as a product. You don't need a 20-person team to start — you need a paved road.

in 𝕏

Ask five developers at the same company how to create a new service and you'll get five answers, three abandoned wiki pages and one Slack thread that ends with "just copy the payments repo". That gap — between writing code and running software the organisation's way — is where weeks of engineering time quietly disappear. An internal developer platform (IDP) closes it.

1. Why platforms, why now

The cloud-native stack won, and it brought real complexity: Kubernetes or serverless, IaC, OIDC, supply-chain scanning, observability, on-call. Expecting every product team to master all of it is how you get snowflake infrastructure and burned-out seniors. The platform team's job is to absorb that complexity once and sell the result internally as self-service capabilities.

A platform is not a mandate. If developers use your paved road because policy forces them to, you built a toll booth. If they use it because it's genuinely the fastest way to ship, you built a platform.

2. The golden path

The core artefact is the golden path: the blessed, documented, automated route from "I have an idea" to "it's running in production with dashboards and alerts". A good first golden path covers:

  • Scaffold — one command/portal click creates the repo from a template: service skeleton, Dockerfile, CI pipeline, IaC module, README.
  • Build & deploy — merging to main deploys to a staging environment with zero configuration; production is one approval away.
  • Operate — logs, metrics, traces and a pre-built dashboard exist from the first deploy; alerts route to the owning team automatically.
  • Own — the service appears in a catalog with its owner, tier, docs and runbook links.

3. A minimum viable platform (no, you don't need to buy everything)

For a 30–150 engineer organisation, this stack gets you 80% of the value:

CapabilityPragmatic choiceNotes
Service catalog + templatesBackstage, or even a well-kept repo of cookiecutter templatesStart with templates; add the portal when templates prove out.
CI/CD paved roadShared, versioned pipeline definitions (reusable workflows)Teams inherit; they don't copy-paste.
InfrastructureA handful of blessed IaC modules"Postgres, queue, web service" covers most requests.
Secrets & identityCloud-native (Key Vault / Secrets Manager) + OIDC everywhereNo long-lived credentials in CI. Ever.
ObservabilityOpenTelemetry baked into the service templateInstrumentation nobody has to remember is instrumentation that exists.

Notice what's not on the list: a custom UI, a Kubernetes distribution of your own, or a year-long re-platforming programme. Two senior engineers and a quarter is a credible v1.

4. Run it as a product, not a project

Platforms fail socially before they fail technically. The fixes are product-management basics applied inward:

  1. Your developers are customers. Interview them, watch them onboard, keep a public roadmap and changelog.
  2. Migrations are your job, not theirs. Every breaking platform change ships with a codemod or a migration PR opened by the platform team.
  3. Escape hatches stay open. Teams with genuinely unusual needs can leave the paved road — they just own what they build there. Document the trade explicitly.

5. Measuring whether it's working

Pick a small set and publish them monthly:

  • Time to first deploy for a brand-new service (the headline platform metric — hours, not weeks).
  • Adoption — % of active services on the paved-road pipeline and templates.
  • DORA basics — deployment frequency and change failure rate, trending by team.
  • Developer satisfaction — a two-question quarterly pulse beats a 40-question annual survey nobody answers.

Platform engineering, done honestly, is an act of empathy backed by automation: noticing the toil every team pays and deciding that someone should pay it once, properly, on everyone's behalf. Start with one golden path, treat your engineers like customers, and let adoption — not mandate — tell you whether you've earned the name "platform".
